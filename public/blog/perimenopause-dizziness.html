Dizzy Spells and Vertigo in Perimenopause — What's Actually Happening

March 12, 20269 min read

The room tilts. You grab the kitchen counter, squeeze your eyes shut, and wait for the world to stop moving. It passes in a few seconds — maybe ten, maybe thirty — but your heart is hammering and your palms are damp. You stand there, perfectly still, wondering: What just happened?

Maybe it's been happening for a few weeks. Maybe months. The dizzy spells come without warning — standing up from your desk, rolling over in bed, bending down to unload the dishwasher. Sometimes the room spins. Other times it's more like a lightheaded, floaty feeling, as if you're slightly disconnected from your own body. You might feel unsteady walking down the hallway, or notice that turning your head quickly makes the world lurch sideways for a beat.

So you go to the doctor. They check your blood pressure. They look in your ears. Maybe they refer you to an ENT or a neurologist. You get an MRI. Everything comes back normal. And you're left standing in a parking lot with a stack of normal test results and a symptom that nobody can explain.

Here's what most of those specialists probably didn't ask you: How old are you, and have your periods changed at all?

The Connection Between Hormones and Your Balance System

Your sense of balance is orchestrated by an incredibly sophisticated system. Your inner ear contains tiny structures called the vestibular organs — the semicircular canals and the otolith organs — that detect motion and gravity. These structures work in concert with your eyes, your proprioceptors (sensors in your muscles and joints), and your brain's vestibular processing centers to keep you upright and oriented in space.

What most people don't realize — and what many doctors don't connect — is that estrogen receptors are present throughout the vestibular system. They've been identified in the inner ear, in the vestibular nerve, and in the brainstem areas that process balance information. This isn't a minor footnote. Estrogen actively participates in how your balance system functions.

Research published in Audiology and Neurotology has demonstrated that estrogen influences blood flow to the inner ear, the composition of the fluid (endolymph) within the vestibular organs, and the firing rate of vestibular nerve cells. When estrogen levels are stable, this system hums along without you ever thinking about it. When estrogen fluctuates dramatically — as it does during perimenopause — the system can become unreliable.

How Estrogen Affects Your Inner Ear

The inner ear is a fluid-filled labyrinth, and the precise composition of that fluid matters enormously. The endolymph — the fluid inside the semicircular canals — has a very specific balance of sodium and potassium ions. Estrogen helps regulate this balance. When estrogen levels swing wildly, as they do during the perimenopausal transition, the ionic concentration of the endolymph may shift, potentially leading to changes in how the vestibular hair cells respond to movement.

Think of it like this: your inner ear is a finely calibrated instrument, and estrogen is one of the substances that keeps it calibrated. When the calibration drifts, even slightly, you can experience anything from mild unsteadiness to full rotational vertigo.

The Blood Flow Factor

Estrogen is also a vasodilator — it helps keep blood vessels relaxed and open. The inner ear is served by very small blood vessels (the labyrinthine artery), and it's extremely sensitive to changes in blood flow. During perimenopause, the loss of estrogen's vasodilatory effect may reduce blood flow to the inner ear, making it more vulnerable to transient ischemia — brief periods of reduced blood supply that can trigger dizziness or vertigo episodes.

This vascular component may also explain why some women find that their dizzy spells are worse during times of cardiovascular stress — after skipping meals, during heart palpitations, or in hot environments. The inner ear simply isn't getting the consistent blood supply it needs to function optimally.

The Otolith Connection

There's a specific type of vertigo called benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) that becomes significantly more common in women during and after the menopausal transition. BPPV occurs when tiny calcium carbonate crystals (otoconia) become dislodged from the otolith organs and migrate into the semicircular canals, where they don't belong. These displaced crystals cause the canals to send false motion signals to your brain, resulting in brief but intense spinning sensations triggered by head position changes.

Research suggests that estrogen helps maintain the structural integrity of the otoconia and the membrane they sit on. As estrogen declines, the otoconia may become more fragile and prone to breaking loose. A study in Menopause found that women with lower estrogen levels had significantly higher rates of BPPV, and that the condition was more likely to recur in postmenopausal women not using hormone therapy.

What Perimenopausal Dizziness Actually Feels Like

Women describe it in different ways, and the variety of descriptions is part of what makes it hard to pin down:

  • Room-spinning vertigo — the classic merry-go-round sensation, often triggered by rolling over in bed or looking up
  • Lightheadedness — a floating, woozy feeling that comes and goes, sometimes lasting minutes, sometimes hours
  • Unsteadiness — feeling like you're walking on a boat, especially in visually busy environments like grocery stores
  • Head pressure with imbalance — a sense of fullness or pressure in the head accompanied by difficulty maintaining balance
  • Postural drops — dizziness when standing up quickly, often worse first thing in the morning
  • Motion sensitivity — increased sensitivity to car rides, scrolling on screens, or watching action movies that never bothered you before

Many women also notice that their dizziness travels with other perimenopausal symptoms. It might worsen alongside anxiety episodes, arrive during the same phase of their cycle that brings hot flashes, or intensify during weeks when electric shock sensations are more frequent. These symptom clusters make more sense when you understand they share a common hormonal driver.

Why Standard Medical Workups Miss This

If you've been through the medical wringer for your dizziness, you probably had some or all of the following: blood pressure checks (sitting and standing), an EKG, blood work for anemia and blood sugar, possibly an MRI of your brain, and maybe a visit to an ENT who performed a Dix-Hallpike maneuver for BPPV.

These are all appropriate tests. They rule out important things — blood pressure dysregulation, cardiac arrhythmias, anemia, brain tumors, and mechanical BPPV. But none of them assess the hormonal environment of your vestibular system. There's no standard test that measures estrogen's effect on inner ear function. So when everything comes back "normal," you're told there's nothing wrong — when in reality, the problem is operating at a level that current standard testing doesn't capture.

This is a pattern that shows up across perimenopausal symptoms. The tests are normal because they're testing for the wrong things. Your heart is structurally fine — it's the hormonal influence on your autonomic nervous system that's causing palpitations. Your brain scan is clean — it's the neurochemical shifts causing your cognitive changes. And your inner ear looks normal under an otoscope — but the fluid dynamics inside it are subtly altered by hormonal fluctuations.

Other Factors That Make Dizziness Worse

The Anxiety-Dizziness Loop

There's a well-documented bidirectional relationship between anxiety and dizziness. Anxiety activates the sympathetic nervous system, which can alter blood flow to the inner ear and increase sensitivity of the vestibular system. Dizziness, in turn, triggers anxiety — because feeling like the world is spinning is inherently frightening. During perimenopause, when both anxiety and vestibular function are affected by the same hormonal changes, this loop can become particularly vicious.

Some women develop what's called persistent postural-perceptual dizziness (PPPD) — a chronic dizziness condition that's maintained by the brain's hypervigilance to balance sensations. It often starts after a triggering event (like a BPPV episode or a period of intense hormonal fluctuation) and persists long after the original trigger has resolved, because the brain has essentially recalibrated to expect the dizziness.

Blood Sugar and Blood Pressure Instability

Perimenopausal hormonal changes can affect insulin sensitivity and blood pressure regulation. Some women develop reactive hypoglycemia (blood sugar drops after eating) or orthostatic intolerance (blood pressure drops when standing) that they never had before. Both can cause dizziness that overlaps with and amplifies the vestibular symptoms.

Migraine and Vestibular Migraine

Vestibular migraine — migraine that primarily manifests as dizziness rather than headache — becomes more common during perimenopause. Estrogen fluctuations are a well-known migraine trigger, and some women who never had migraines before develop vestibular migraine during the hormonal transition. This can present as episodes of vertigo lasting minutes to hours, sometimes with light or sound sensitivity, and often without any headache at all.

What Actually Helps

1. Track the Pattern

Start logging your dizzy episodes alongside your menstrual cycle, sleep quality, meals, and stress levels. Even four to six weeks of data can reveal patterns that help you and your healthcare provider make connections. Many women discover that their worst dizziness episodes cluster around the same time in their cycle — typically when estrogen is falling most rapidly.

2. Vestibular Rehabilitation

Vestibular rehabilitation therapy (VRT) is a specialized form of physical therapy that trains your brain to compensate for vestibular dysfunction. It involves specific eye and head movement exercises designed to reduce dizziness and improve balance. Research shows it's effective for many types of dizziness, and it may be particularly helpful for perimenopausal women whose vestibular system is being challenged by hormonal changes. Ask for a referral to a vestibular physiotherapist.

3. Address the Hormonal Root

If your dizziness correlates clearly with your cycle or with other perimenopausal symptoms, discuss hormone therapy with a menopause-informed provider. Some research suggests that stabilizing estrogen levels may improve vestibular symptoms, particularly for women who also have vestibular migraine or recurrent BPPV. This is an area where treatment needs to be individualized.

4. Manage Contributing Factors

Dehydration worsens dizziness significantly. During perimenopause, many women don't drink enough water, especially if they're trying to manage hot flashes by avoiding hot beverages. Aim for at least eight glasses of water daily. Reduce caffeine if it triggers or worsens your episodes. Stabilize blood sugar by eating regular meals with protein and healthy fats — long gaps between meals can trigger lightheadedness.

5. Address Sleep and Stress

Poor sleep amplifies vestibular sensitivity. If you're struggling to sleep through the night, addressing that may improve your dizziness more than any other single intervention. Chronic stress keeps your sympathetic nervous system in overdrive, which directly worsens vestibular symptoms. Regular mindfulness practice, even ten minutes daily, has been shown to reduce dizziness severity in clinical studies.

6. Safety Strategies

While you're working on the underlying causes, protect yourself from falls. Get up slowly from bed and chairs. Use a nightlight in the bathroom. Be cautious on stairs, especially when tired. Remove trip hazards from your home. These practical steps matter — falls due to dizziness are a real risk, and preventing one is easier than recovering from one.

When to Seek Urgent Care

While perimenopausal dizziness is common and generally not dangerous, certain presentations require urgent medical evaluation. Seek immediate care if your dizziness is accompanied by sudden severe headache, slurred speech, vision loss, facial drooping, weakness on one side of your body, difficulty walking, or sudden hearing loss. These can indicate stroke or other serious conditions that need immediate treatment.

Also see your doctor promptly if your dizziness is constant (not episodic), getting progressively worse over weeks, or accompanied by hearing loss on one side. These patterns warrant further investigation beyond the standard perimenopausal picture.

You're Not Making It Up

Dizziness is one of those symptoms that can make you feel like you're losing your mind — partly because it's invisible, partly because tests often come back normal, and partly because it's genuinely disorienting to feel like you can't trust your own balance. Add to that the fact that many doctors don't connect dizziness to hormonal changes, and you've got a recipe for feeling dismissed and confused.

But the science is clear: estrogen plays a meaningful role in vestibular function, and perimenopausal hormonal fluctuations can absolutely disrupt your balance system. You're not imagining it. You're not anxious (well, you might be — that's hormonal too). Your body is navigating a legitimate neurological and physiological shift, and the dizziness is a real symptom of a real process.

Understanding what's happening is the first step toward feeling steady again — both physically and emotionally. You don't have to white-knuckle your way through this. There are explanations, there are strategies, and there is help.
